# Inexperienced Dungeon Master Review Log

## 1. Scope of this Agent
You are the **Inexperienced Dungeon Master**. Your job is to:

1. Read the relevant source material in `SourceMaterial/Curse of Stahd Book Markdown/` for any section you review.
2. Read the Writer’s corresponding campaign file(s) in `01-Campaign/`.
3. Judge how easy it would be for a brand‑new, low‑confidence DM (weak actor, easily flustered) to actually *run* those pages at the table.
4. Log your thoughts here, in clear, practical language.
5. Update `agents/progress.md` with scores for **Inexperienced DM** for each section you review.

Your focus is **not** on rules‑accuracy or lore‑accuracy (the Master DM and Editor do that), but on *usability* for a new DM.

---

## 2. Scoring Rubric (Inexperienced Dungeon Master)
For each section in `agents/progress.md`, give a score from **1–10**:

- **1–3 (Very Hard to Run):**
  - Lots of implied knowledge, unexplained rules, or missing steps.
  - DM would need to improvise major things (dialogue, outcomes, descriptions).
  - Little or no guidance on what to say or do next.

- **4–6 (Usable but Rough):**
  - Core flow is there, but some scenes are vague or intimidating for a shy/weak actor.
  - Some moments assume DM confidence in improv or rules.
  - Player choices sometimes lead to “I don’t know what happens next” moments.

- **7–8 (Good for a New DM):**
  - Clear step‑by‑step structure: what to read, what to say, what happens.
  - Plenty of boxed narration, example dialogue, and likely player questions with answers.
  - The DM always has at least one safe, simple option for “what happens next.”

- **9–10 (Excellent for a New DM):**
  - Extremely hand‑holding: can be run almost verbatim by a nervous DM.
  - Includes tone notes, acting tips, safety valves when players go off‑script.
  - Anticipates confusion and addresses it in plain language.

Sections are considered **complete** only when:
- All four reviewing roles have given **5+**, **and**
- The **average score is 7+**.

## 3. Review: 01-Into-the-Mists-and-Death-House

**Files Reviewed:**
- `01-Campaign/01-Into-the-Mists-and-Death-House.md`
- Relevant parts of `SourceMaterial/Curse of Stahd Book Markdown/` (Death House & intro to Barovia)

### 3.1 Ease-of-Use Summary

- The file is **highly structured**, with numbered headings and clear subsections.
- There is **boxed narration** a new DM can read word‑for‑word.
- There are **acting notes and voice guidance** for major NPCs and even for atmospheric elements (the mists, house, etc.).
- The document anticipates **likely player actions** and provides responses and DM guidance.
- Combat encounters and saving throws are **clearly labeled**, with monster lists and battlefield notes.
- There are **prompts** like “What do you do?” and even deliberate **silence prompts**.
- There are notes on **loot** and how/when to award it.

For a brand‑new, weak‑actor DM, this is **very runnable**. They can literally follow the script in order without much improvisation.

### 3.2 Pain Points for a New DM

1. **Length and density.**
   - The detail level is excellent but may feel overwhelming. Consider adding a 1‑page “at-a-glance” flow summary at the top of the file so a new DM doesn’t get lost.

2. **Cross‑referencing.**
   - Sometimes the text refers to things explained later. A new DM might panic if they haven’t pre‑read everything carefully. Occasional “You don’t need to decide this now; see section X.Y later” reminders would help.

3. **Rules comfort assumptions.**
   - A few spots assume the DM knows how to run basic checks, initiative, or skill calls. These are standard for 5e, but a truly fresh DM might like explicit cues such as “Ask for a DC 13 Wisdom (Perception) check from anyone examining the room.” The file mostly does this, but adding it everywhere would push the usability to a 10.

4. **Off‑script player choices.**
   - The document covers **many** likely responses, but not all. Adding one catch‑all “If they try something totally unexpected…” paragraph in each major scene would help reassure new DMs.

### 3.3 Strengths for a New DM

- Very strong **voice guidance**: how to sound, how to pace, how to be creepy without over‑acting.
- Lots of **explicit dialogue** for NPCs and environmental “voices.”
- Good **scene framing** using all five senses.
- Multiple **Strahd interaction options** are clearly marked and give the DM freedom to choose how often to use them.
- Optional side content is **clearly identified**, helping the DM know what is required vs. extra.

### 3.4 Score for 01-Into-the-Mists-and-Death-House

**Inexperienced DM Score: 9 / 10**

Rationale: Exceptionally hand‑holding and structured. The only missing piece for a perfect 10 is a very short “scene flow cheat sheet” and a few more “if players go wild, here’s a safe fallback” notes.

## 4. Review: 02-Village-of-Barovia-and-First-Night

**Files Reviewed:**
- `01-Campaign/02-Village-of-Barovia-and-First-Night.md`
- Relevant parts of `SourceMaterial/Curse of Stahd Book Markdown/` (Village of Barovia, Ismark, Ireena, first night in Barovia)

### 4.1 Ease-of-Use Summary

- This file is **much less developed** than the Death House file.
- Structure and numbered headings are present, but many sections are basically placeholders.
- There is **far less ready-to-read narration** and limited example dialogue.
- Key NPCs (Ismark, Ireena, villagers, tavern keepers) lack the same deep acting notes that the Death House has.

A brand‑new, shy DM would have to **improvise a lot** here, which breaks the design goal of “follow this verbatim.”

### 4.2 Pain Points for a New DM

1. **Missing detailed narration.**
   - The arrival into the village and first impressions need the same level of sensory detail as the mists and Death House.

2. **Sparse NPC guidance.**
   - Voices, mannerisms, and sample conversations for Ismark, Ireena, tavern patrons, and random villagers are minimal or absent.

3. **Unclear flow of the first night.**
   - A nervous DM needs a step‑by‑step of what likely happens: meet X, then Y, then where they can sleep, what might attack, what Strahd might do, etc.

4. **Fewer player‑response branches.**
   - The text doesn’t yet anticipate many of the obvious player choices (e.g., “We go straight to the church,” “We ignore Ismark,” “We try to leave the village immediately”).

### 4.3 Strengths for a New DM

- The intent and structure are clearly modeled on the strong approach of the first file.
- Once fully written to the same standard as Death House, this will likely be very usable.

### 4.4 Score for 02-Village-of-Barovia-and-First-Night

**Inexperienced DM Score: 4 / 10**

Rationale: As of this review, it’s more of an outline than a full hand-holding script. A new DM would struggle to run this purely from the page without lots of improvisation.

## 5. Next-Step Suggestions for the Writer (From an Inexperienced DM’s View)

1. **Match Death House’s Level of Detail.**
   - Use 01-Into-the-Mists-and-Death-House as your template for how dense and explicit Village of Barovia and later chapters should be.

2. **Add “Scene Flow Summaries.”**
   - At the top of each file, include a small numbered list of the 5–10 most likely scenes in order, so a nervous DM can see the whole night/chapter at a glance.

3. **Default Fallbacks.**
   - For each scene, add a short “If players totally derail things, do this simple fallback” paragraph. That kind of safety net is huge for new DMs.

4. **Repeat Key Patterns.**
   - Keep using: boxed narration → acting notes → sample dialogue → likely player actions → DM responses → reminders of any mechanical bits (checks, saves, encounters).
